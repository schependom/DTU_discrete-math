## 9.1 Quotients of polynomial rings with coefficients in a finite field

Using the Isomorphism Theorem for Rings, we can reformulate Proposition 7.1.15, which said that a **non-zero** element $a \in \Zn$ is a unit $\Leftrightarrow \gcd(a,n) = 1$, and is a zero divisor $\Leftrightarrow \gcd(a,n) > 1$:

> [!proposition] Proposition 9.1.1: units and zero divisors in $\Zn$ (p183)
> Let $n \in \Z_{>0}, a \in \Z$. Then **exactly one** of the following three cases holds:
>
> 1. $a + n\Z$ is the **zero element** in $\Z/n\Z$, equivalently: $\gcd(a,n) = n$,
> 2. $a + n\Z$ is a **zero-divisor** in $\Z/n\Z$, equivalently: $1 < \gcd(a,n) < n$,
> 3. $a + n\Z$ is a **unit** in $\Z/n\Z$, equivalently: $\gcd(a,n) = 1$.

A similar proposition holds for quotient rings of polynomial rings with coefficients in a field:

> [!proposition] Proposition 9.1.2: units and zero divisors in $\F[X]/(p(x))$ (p184)
> Let $(\F, +, \cdot)$ be a field, and suppose that $f(X) \in \F[X]$ is a non-constant polynomial of degree $\boxed{d \geq 1}$. Further let $g(X) \in \F[X]$ be an arbitrary polynomial. Then **exactly one** of the following three cases holds:
>
> 1. $g(X) + \gen{f(X)} = 0 + \gen{f(X)}$, the **zero element** in $\F[X]/\gen{f(X)}$.
>    $$\boxed{\deg(\gcd(g(X), f(X))) = d}$$
> 2. $g(X) + \gen{f(X)}$ is a **zero-divisor** in $\F[X]/\gen{f(X)}$.
>    $$\boxed{0 < \deg(\gcd(g(X), f(X))) < d}$$
> 3. $g(X) + \gen{f(X)}$ is a **unit** in $\F[X]/\gen{f(X)}$.
>    $$\boxed{\deg(\gcd(g(X), f(X))) = 0} \iff \gcd(g(X),f(X))=1$$

> [!exam] Facts about the GCD
>
> $$
> \boxed{m(X) := \gcd(g(X), f(X))} \implies \exists p_1, p_2 \in \F[X] : \begin{cases}\boxed{f(X) = m(X) \cdot p_1(X)}\\ \boxed{g(X) = m(X) \cdot p_2(X)}\end{cases}
> $$
>
> and from the E.E.A. we know that $\exists r(X), s(X) \in \F[X]$ such that
>
> $$
> \boxed{m(X) = r(X) \cdot f(X) + s(X) \cdot g(X)}
> $$

> [!proposition] Multiplicative inverse of a unit $g(X) + \gen{f(X)}$ in $\F[X]/\gen{f(X)}$
> From the proof on Blackboard 3, it follows that
>
> $$
> \begin{align*}
> m(X) = 1 &\implies g(X) + \gen{f(X)} \text{ is a unit in } \F[X]/\gen{f(X)}\\
> &\implies \boxed{\big(g(X) + \gen{f(X)}\big)^{-1} = s(X) + \gen{f(X)}}
> \end{align*}
> $$
>
> So the **multiplicative inverse of a unit** $g(X) + \gen{f(X)}$ is given by $$\boxed{s(X) + \gen{f(X)}}$$where $s(X)$ is obtained from the Extended Euclidean Algorithm such that $$1 = r(X) \cdot f(X) + s(X) \cdot g(X)$$

> [!fact]
> Given a factorization $f(X) = f_1(X) \cdot f_2(X)$ we can see that $$\boxed{\begin{cases}f_1(X) + \gen{f(X)} \\ \qquad \text{ and } \\ f_2(X) + \gen{f(X)} \end{cases} \text{ are zero divisors in } \F[X]/\gen{f(X)}}$$

> \begin{align*}
> \boxed{\big(f_1(X) + \gen{f(X)}\big) \cdot \big(f_2(X) + \gen{f(X)}\big)} &= f_1(X) \cdot f_2(X) + \gen{f(X)}\\
> &= f(X) + \gen{f(X)}
> \\&= \boxed{0 + \gen{f(X)}}
> \end{align*}
> $$
>
> Where of course neither $f_1(X) + \gen{f(X)}$ nor $f_2(X) + \gen{f(X)}$ is equal to $0 + \gen{f(X)}$. To check this, note that if for example $f_1(X) + \gen{f(X)} = 0 + \gen{f(X)}$, then $f_1(X) \in \gen{f(X)}$, so there exists a polynomial $q(X) \in \F[X]$ such that $$f_1(X) = f(X) \cdot q(X)$$ But this is impossible since $\deg(f_1(X)) < \deg(f(X))$.

## 9.2 Construction of fields using irreducible polynomials

We saw already a field $(\Z_p, +_p, \cdot_p) = (\F_p, +, \cdot)$ can be constructed as the quotient ring $\Z/p\Z$ where $p$ is a prime number.

Another procedure to produce fields is by considering **quotient rings** of $\F[X]$, the polynomial ring with coefficients in a field $\F$. We will need the **analogue of prime numbers for polynomials**, which are called **irreducible polynomials**.

> [!definition] Factors of a polynomial
> Let $f(X) \in \F[X]$ be a polynomial. If we can write
>
> $$
> \boxed{f(X) = g(X) \cdot h(X)}
> $$
>
> for some polynomials $g(X), h(X) \in \F[X]$, then we say that $g(X)$ and $h(X)$ are **factors** of $f(X)$.
>
> We call $g(X)$ a **proper factor** of $f(X)$ if
>
> $$
> \boxed{0 < \deg(g(X)) < \deg(f(X))}
> $$

> [!definition] Definition irreducible polynomial (p186)
> Let $(\F, +, \cdot)$ be a field. A polynomial $f(X) \in \F[X]$ is called **irreducible** if
> $$\boxed{\deg(f(X)) \geq 1 \text{ and } f(X) \text{ has no proper factors}}$$
> It is only divisible by itself or by constant polynomials (degree 0):
>
> $$
> \boxed{f(X) = g(X) \cdot h(X) \implies \begin{cases}\deg(g(X)) = 0\\ \textit{ or}\\ \deg(h(X)) = 0\end{cases}}
> $$

> [!definition] Definition 9.2.1: irreducible polynomial (p186)
> Let $(\F, +, \cdot)$ be a field. A polynomial $f(X) \in \F[X]$ is called **irreducible** if
>
> -   it has positive degree: $\deg(f(X)) \geq 1$
> -   if for $g(X), h(X) \in \F[X]$:$$\boxed{f(X) = g(X) \cdot h(X) \implies \begin{cases}\deg(g(X)) = \deg(f(X)) \\ \text{ or}\\ \deg(h(X)) = \deg(f(X))\end{cases}}$$

_BECAUSE RECALL_: In a **domain**, $\forall p(X), q(X) \in R[X]: \deg(p(X) \cdot q(X)) = \deg(p(X)) + \deg(q(X))$.
So, in fields (which are $\subseteq$ domains):$$\boxed{\deg(g(X)\cdot h(X)) = \deg(g(X)) + \deg(h(X))}$$

It is easy to check whether a polynomial of degree at most 3 is irreducible.

> [!lemma] Lemma 9.2.4: Irreducibility of 1-degree polynomials (p186)
> Let $(\F, +, \cdot)$ be a field. Any polynomial $f(X) \in \F[X]$ of degree 1 is irreducible.

> [!lemma] Lemma 9.2.5: Irreducibility of 2- and 3-degree polynomials (p186)
> Let $(\F, +, \cdot)$ be a field. Let $f(X) \in \F[X]$ be a polynomial with $\deg(f(X)) \in \{2,3\}$. Then
>
> $$
> \boxed{f(X) \text{ is irreducible} \iff f(X) \text{ has no roots in } \F}
> $$

> [!exam] Larger degrees
> To find an irreducible polynomial of degree 4 it is **necessary**, **but not sufficient** that the polynomial has no roots!
>
> $$
> \boxed{f(X) \text{ has degree } 2 \text{ or } 3 \text{ and } f(X) \text{ is reducible} \implies f(X) \text{ has a root in } \F}
> $$

> [!theorem] Thereom 9.2.12: Construction of fields (p190)
> Let $(\F, +, \cdot)$ be a field and let $f(X) \in \F[X]$ be a non-constant polynomial.
> Then the quotient ring $$\boxed{(\F[X]/\gen{f(X)}, \cdot, +) \text{ is a field} \iff f(X) \text{ is irreducible}}$$

We can see $\F$ is a subset of $\E := \F[X]/\gen{f(X)}$ by identifying each element $a \in \F$ with the coset $a + \gen{f(X)} \in \E$.

$$
\boxed{\psi : \begin{cases}\F \to \E\\ a \mapsto a + \gen{f(X)}\end{cases} \quad \text{ is an injective ring homomorphism}}
$$

> [!definition] Notation: extension field (p190)
> Let $(\F, +, \cdot)$ be a field and let $f(X) \in \F[X]$ be an **irreducible polynomial**.
> Then we define the **extension field**
>
> $$
> \boxed{\E := \F[X]/\gen{f(X)}}
> $$
>
> of $\F$ by $f(X)$. We call $\F$ a **subfield** of $\E$.

> [!lemma] Lemma 9.2.13 (p190)
> Let $f(X) \in \F[X]$ be an irreducible polynomial and define $\E := \F[X]/\gen{f(X)}$.
> Then the element $X + \gen{f(X)} \in \E$ is a root of $f(T)$ when seen as an element of the set of polynomials $\E[T]$
